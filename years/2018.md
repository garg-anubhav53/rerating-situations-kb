# 2018 — Re-Rating Situations

**Thesis of the year:** 2018 was a **DOWN, risk-off year** — the S&P 500 fell **-6.2%** (its first negative
year since 2008), the Nasdaq -3.9%, with a brutal Q4 selloff (S&P -14% Oct–Dec) that trimmed gains built up
earlier in the year. In a hostile tape where **only ~160 of 505 S&P names even finished positive and the #1
S&P performer (AMD) rose just +73%**, a clean **≥2x within the calendar year was extraordinarily rare** — and
essentially **NO large/mega-cap doubled.** The ≥2x cohort was tiny and concentrated in two legible engines:

1. **The cannabis IPO/legalization mania** — the year's signature bubble. Canada legalized recreational
   cannabis (Oct 17 2018), the first pure-play cannabis names listed on major US exchanges, and a wall of
   retail money chased them. **TLRY/Tilray** (IPO'd $17 July → ~$300 intraday Sept 19 short-squeeze →
   ~$70 close, still ~4x IPO) was the archetype; **CRON/Cronos** (Altria's $1.8B/45% stake, Dec 7),
   **IIPR** (the durable "picks-and-shovels" cannabis REIT), **MRMD/MariMed** (nano roll-up, biggest
   cannabis penny-stock gainer of the year), and ancillary **KSHB/GRWG** rode it. Big-cap Canadian LPs
   **CGC/Canopy (~1.9x, just short)** and **ACB/Aurora (+9%)** did NOT clear a clean 2x — the mania paid the
   *smaller* pure-plays and the newly-listed floats hardest.
2. **Biotech clinical de-risk + M&A takeouts** — the other place a specific catalyst re-priced a specific
   asset in a down tape: **ECYT/Endocyte** (~6-8x, Lu-PSMA radioligand revival → Novartis $2.1B buyout Dec),
   **AMRN/Amarin** (~3.4x, REDUCE-IT Vascepa CV-outcomes trial — the cleanest business-change re-rate of the
   year), **ARWR/Arrowhead** (~3-4x, RNAi/TRiM platform reboot), plus takeout doublers **TSRO/Tesaro**
   (GSK $5.1B) and **ABLX/Ablynx** (Sanofi €3.9B). A couple of growth/IPO names bucked the tape:
   **TTD/The Trade Desk** (+154%, best full-year return of any major US stock in 2018) and **ZS/Zscaler**
   (cloud-security IPO). Note the marquee *near-miss*: **MDGL/Madrigal** put up the year's most dramatic
   biotech chart — its MGL-3196 (resmetirom) NASH Phase 2 data (+121% on May 31) spiked it ~3.3x intra-year
   into the low-$300s — but it **round-tripped to a $112.72 Dec-31 close (~+22% on the year off a ~$92
   Jan open), FAILING the clean calendar-2018 ≥2x bar** and so is excluded (the same point-to-point-laundering
   pattern as TLRY; see Excluded).

**Dominant split:** in a bear year the ≥2x names cleave the usual way — **genuine business-change** re-rates
(AMRN's outcomes-trial, MDGL's Phase-2 data, ECYT/TSRO/ABLX validated by acquirer cash, VCEL's commercial
execution) that were *earned*, vs. **narrative/flow on newly-listed floats** (TLRY's $300 round-trip, the
cannabis-mania tail) that were *borrowed*. 2018's lesson: **when the whole market is falling, a double is a
loud signal — it means a real, asset-specific catalyst overwhelmed a -20% market beta.** The cannabis cohort
is the anti-lesson (peak-to-close round-trips inside the same year); the takeout/data names are the durable ones.

**Coverage note:** the international/EM and micro/OTC buckets were **materially under-covered this run**
(repeated 403 walls on macrotrends/stockanalysis/OTC-Markets screeners; engines return current-not-2018 data).
Asia (Japan/Korea/Taiwan/China/India small-cap), Brazil (H2-2018 Bolsonaro-election Ibovespa rally), and the
2018 OTCQX Best-50 roster remain a genuine gap → year left **PARTIAL** for a dedicated micro/international
deep-sweep (which will skip the SEEN names captured here).

### ARWR — Arrowhead Pharmaceuticals

**2018 re-rating: ~3-4x (H1 2018 reported +346%; stock ran from roughly $3-6 toward $15-20+ before a Q4 market-wide selloff trimmed gains — exact year-end retention unverified).** Arrowhead is an RNAi (RNA-interference) platform biotech whose original delivery vehicle, EX-1/DPC, suffered a fatal-toxicity signal in a 2016 trial that forced the company to scrap its entire clinical pipeline and rebuild almost from scratch. The stock was left for dead — priced as though the underlying RNAi science, not just the delivery chemistry, had failed. What the market missed was that Arrowhead's core insight (targeting specific liver mRNAs) was sound; only the delivery mechanism was broken. Through 2017-2018 the company rebuilt around a new subcutaneously-administered **TRiM (Targeted RNAi Molecule)** platform, and in 2018 delivered the first clinical proof: **ARO-AAT** (alpha-1 antitrypsin liver disease) advanced through Phase 1 with encouraging knockdown data (up to ~92% AAT suppression seen preclinically), alongside early cardio-metabolic candidates — validating that the new chemistry actually worked in patients, not just in animal models.

The business change was real and platform-wide: a working subcutaneous delivery system meant every asset built on TRiM — not just ARO-AAT — was suddenly de-risked simultaneously, which is why the market re-rated the whole pipeline rather than just one drug. This also set up the credibility that attracted major pharma validation (Janssen/J&J and Amgen platform licensing deals followed in late 2018 into 2019, just after this window). The transferable lesson: in platform biotech, when a *delivery* or safety failure — not the underlying science — kills the stock, a fixed-delivery reboot can re-rate the entire pipeline at once, because the market was pricing in platform death, not asset-by-asset risk.

### ECYT — Endocyte (NASDAQ: ECYT)

**Headline:** ECYT, NASDAQ. From a ~$3 low (52-week low of $2.81 in early 2018) to the $24.00/share Novartis cash takeout — roughly a **6–8x** re-rating over calendar 2018, starting from a market cap of roughly **$200–260M** and ending in an **~$2.1B** all-cash acquisition that closed December 2018. This is not a "the story got hot" re-rating. It is the cleaner, rarer kind: someone else's balance sheet showed up and paid cash to settle the argument.

**What the market misunderstood.** By 2018, Endocyte was, to most of the market, a corpse that hadn't finished falling over. The company's entire identity for a decade had been **folate-targeting** — using folate conjugates to deliver payloads to folate-receptor-overexpressing tumors. The lead asset, **vintafolide**, was partnered with Merck in platinum-resistant ovarian cancer. In **May 2014** the Phase 3 **PROCEED** trial was halted for futility; the stock **collapsed ~62% in a single session**, Merck walked away, and the European filing was withdrawn. From that point Endocyte was filed under "busted single-asset biotech." That filing-away is exactly what the market got wrong. On **October 2, 2017**, Endocyte licensed **177Lu-PSMA-617**, a **Phase-3-ready PSMA-targeted radioligand therapy** for metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer, with striking compassionate-use data (PSA declines >50% in ~40–60% of heavily pre-treated mCRPC patients). PSMA is expressed in ~80% of mCRPC patients — an enormous addressable population. The misunderstanding had two layers: the ticker's *reputation* still priced the vintafolide obituary while the *pipeline* had re-tooled into a radiopharma name; and the **modality itself was being validated at that very moment** — Novartis's own **177Lu-DOTATATE (Lutathera)** for neuroendocrine tumors was **approved January 2018**. Endocyte wasn't a busted folate shell; it was a cheap, listed way to own the second — and much larger — indication of an emerging blockbuster modality.

**What actually changed — business vs re-rating.** *Business change (real but incremental in 2018):* the international Phase 3 **VISION** trial in PSMA-positive mCRPC launched and enrolled; the radioligand class got external air-cover from Lutathera's approval (de-risking isotope supply, regulatory precedent, reimbursement); an early-2018 equity raise funded VISION through readout, plus an early adaptor-CAR-T program as optionality. Crucially, **VISION did NOT read out in 2018** — the pivotal proof came years later. *Re-rating change (the part that mattered):* on **October 18, 2018**, **Novartis agreed to acquire Endocyte for $24.00/share cash — ~$2.1B, a ~54% premium** over the prior $15.56 close and a multiple of the January low. This was **acquirer-validation in its purest form**: Novartis had just launched Lutathera, owned the isotope infrastructure and the conviction, and was in effect buying the large second indication to sit beside the small first one. The re-rating ran *ahead* of the science and was then ratified by a strategic buyer's balance sheet rather than by a data readout.

**Mechanism & timeline.** May 2014 PROCEED halt (−62%, Merck exits) → Oct 2 2017 Lu-PSMA-617 in-license (market treats it as a lottery ticket on a shell) → Jan 2018 Lutathera FDA approval credentials the class while ECYT sits near its $2.81 low → early–mid 2018 VISION enrolls, equity raise, stock climbs into the teens as specialists re-underwrite it as a theranostics leader → Oct 17 2018 prior close $15.56 → **Oct 18 2018 Novartis $24/share, ~$2.1B, ~54% premium** → Dec 2018 deal closes, ECYT delisted, ending the year re-rated ~6–8x into the takeout. Postscript: 177Lu-PSMA-617 became **Pluvicto**, FDA-approved March 2022, a multi-billion-dollar franchise — the 2018 buyer's thesis vindicated in full.

**Transferable lesson.** (1) **Buy a validated *modality* cheaply through a busted-drug shell** — the mispricing lives entirely in the lag between the ticker's dead-asset reputation and its re-tooled pipeline, and the catalyst that closes the lag can be a *competitor's* approval (Lutathera) that de-risks the whole class. (2) **Acquirer-validation is the durable form of re-rating** — a takeout premium is a mark-to-transaction, not a mark-to-model; when the best-informed party (Novartis, already inside the modality) pays a 54% cash premium *before* the pivotal data, the re-rating stops being an opinion. The tell was structural and available *ex ante*: the natural acquirer was obvious — whoever launches the first drug in a class will pay up for the largest indication in it. (3) **Themes compound into M&A waves** — Endocyte was the opening chapter of a multi-year radiopharma consolidation (later RayzeBio/BMS, POINT/Lilly, RYZ101); own the cheapest legitimate exposure to a modality's *next* indication at the moment of its first credentialing event, because that is exactly what large caps without in-house capability will be forced to buy. The uncomfortable corollary: **the money in ECYT 2018 was made before the science was proven** — the durable re-rate came from correctly reading *who would need to own this and when*, which is why it held where narrative-only names round-tripped.

### VCEL — Vericel

**Vericel Corporation (NASDAQ: VCEL)** re-rated roughly 3x in 2018 (Jan–early Dec 2018, cited +240%; started ~$7–8/share, market cap climbing from the small-cap floor to roughly $270–300M) (unverified: precise price/cap figures) — with essentially no single catalyst driving the move. Vericel is a cell-therapy/regenerative-medicine commercial-stage company built on two products: **MACI**, an autologous cultured chondrocyte implant for knee cartilage defects, and **Epicel**, a permanent skin replacement for severe burns. The market had priced VCEL as a former cell-therapy also-ran with a slow, uncertain commercial ramp — the kind of story that stays off screens because there's no binary readout to trade. What actually changed was the P&L: MACI revenue growth accelerated sharply through the year (Q1 2018 total revenue +93% y/y, MACI +141% y/y; Q3 2018 revenue $22.5M with gross margin expanding to 64% from 50% a year earlier), driven by an expanded sales force (28→40 reps), added manufacturing capacity, and a broadened pharmacy distribution network. Guidance was raised repeatedly (full-year product revenue guided to $73–78M vs. $62.8M in 2017), the company reported record quarterly revenue, net income, and operating cash flow in Q3, and closed 2018 with its first full-year positive adjusted EBITDA — a genuine operating-leverage inflection, not just top-line noise.

The re-rating was multiple expansion layered on top of that fundamental inflection: as beat-and-raise quarters accumulated, the market gradually re-classified VCEL from "speculative cell-therapy name" to "profitable, scaling commercial compounder," and the multiple caught up to the earnings trajectory rather than jumping on a single event. The transferable lesson is that quiet commercial-execution compounders — re-rates built on sequential beat-and-raise quarters rather than a single catalyst (FDA decision, M&A, data readout) — tend to be less crowded and more durable than event-driven biotech trades, precisely because they lack the headline that draws in fast money early; VCEL continued compounding for several years after 2018 as the market kept catching up to the fundamentals.

### MRMD — MariMed

MariMed was a sub-$50M OTC nano-cap that spent 2018 as the biggest gainer in the cannabis penny-stock complex — reported up as much as +474% year-to-date by early December (unverified), and by at least one account +371% for the full year, outpacing even Tilray's headline-grabbing +215% (Motley Fool). The stock ran from low-single-digits to an all-time intraday high of $5.80 on October 8, 2018 (closing high $5.38 on October 5), before round-tripping into a December double-bottom near $2.35 — call it roughly 4-5x peak-to-trough on the year, though exact January 2 / December 31 closes are unverified. What made this one strange rather than just another cannabis-mania flyer is that MariMed wasn't a story stock with a pitch deck and no plant-touching business: it was a real, if obscure, multi-state roll-up — developing and managing licensed cultivation/processing facilities in Massachusetts, Illinois, Delaware, Maryland, and Nevada under real-estate, licensing, and consulting agreements, while building its own branded product lines (Nature's Heritage flower/vapes, Betty's Eddies gummies, Bubby's Baked goods, Vibations drink mix). None of that got press-cycle attention next to the big Canadian LPs; the market was chasing Tilray and Canopy while a tiny OTC name was quietly stitching together actual state-by-state infrastructure and licensing cash flow.

The re-rating itself, though, was less about anyone correctly modeling that infrastructure buildout and more about mechanics: a genuine (if underappreciated) business-improvement story collided with an illiquid, thinly-floated OTC ticker at the peak of 2018's cannabis-legalization mania, and low float did what low float does — it amplified inflows into a parabolic move, then amplified the reversal into the December pullback. The transferable lesson: the most extreme multiples in a thematic mania tend to come from the un-narrated micro/OTC pure-play compounding off a tiny base, not the names already priced for the story — but a low-float OTC structure is simultaneously the engine of that upside and the mechanism of the round trip, so before treating a MRMD-style chart as validation, verify there's real revenue-generating substance (facilities, licenses, brands) underneath the move rather than pure float-driven pump.

### TLRY — Tilray, Inc. (NASDAQ: TLRY)

**Headline:** IPO July 19, 2018 at **$17.00** → intraday peak **~$300 on Sept 19, 2018** (close $214.06) → year-end close **~$70** (unverified to the dollar). That's a **~17–18x** melt-up from IPO to intraday peak in nine weeks, a **-75%+ peak-to-close** collapse, and still a **~4x** gain on the IPO price for the calendar year. IPO raised ~$153M on 10.35M shares at $17; the true tell was the **float**: Privateer Holdings, the pre-IPO parent, held the vast majority of the ~90M+ shares outstanding, leaving only the ~9M primary shares (plus greenshoe) truly tradeable — a single-digit-to-teens-of-millions free float against a company that briefly carried a **~$20B+ headline market cap** on essentially no revenue. This is the archetypal 2018 cannabis-mania name and the cleanest micro-float round-trip in the KB.

**What the market misunderstood.** It fused two unrelated things and priced them as one: (1) a **legalization TAM story** (Canada launched the first G7 federal rec market on Oct 17, 2018; analysts extrapolated "next tobacco/alcohol"), and (2) a **tiny tradeable float**. What people bid on in September was not "Canadian cannabis TAM" — it was a handful of freely-floating shares into which retail demand, momentum funds, and a crowded short book all tried to transact at once. The error was treating a **supply/demand technical as fundamental value**: a stock is priced at the margin by whoever is forced to transact, and when marginal supply is a few million shares and marginal demand includes shorts who *must* cover, the clearing price detaches completely from any DCF. The headline "$20B market cap" was itself a phantom — a squeeze-set marginal price multiplied by a mostly-locked-up share count that could never have been monetized near that level.

**What actually changed — business vs re-rating.** *Business change: essentially none.* Tilray in 2018 was a small medical-cannabis producer with ~$43M full-year revenue, loss-making, no proven cost advantage or defensible moat; the Sept 18 DEA import approval was a single research-supply shipment for a UCSD trial (a genuine "first," but immaterial to revenue), and Canadian legalization lifted the *whole* sector (and heralded the 2019–20 oversupply glut that gutted margins). *Re-rating change: ~100% of it* — a flow-and-mechanics event. The gears: **micro-float short squeeze** (scarce/expensive borrow, any buying forced shorts to cover into no supply — Zacks literally titled its Sept 19 piece "Anatomy of a Short Squeeze"), **retail reflexivity** (price → coverage → more buying → price), **options gamma** (call-buying forced dealer hedging that amplified the up-move in a thin float), and a **stacked narrative** (DEA approval + imminent legalization + CEO Brendan Kennedy on CNBC/Cramer at the peak). The business barely moved; the re-rating was the whole event.

**Mechanism & timeline.** July 19 IPO at $17, closes day-1 ~$22 → late-July→mid-Sept grind up as legalization approaches and lockups keep ~75M+ Privateer shares off the market → Sept 18 DEA-import headline → **Sept 19 blow-off to intraday $300 (+~90%), five Nasdaq volatility halts, closes $214 (+38%)** as forced buyers (shorts) exhaust with no fundamental bid underneath → late-Sept→Dec collapse (−34% in October alone, sell-the-news post-legalization, lockup overhang) to ~$70 close. Named gears: short-cover buying × dealer gamma hedging × momentum/retail inflow × fixed micro-float × narrative excuse; when the forced-buyer fuel exhausted, every gear reversed.

**Transferable lesson.** The pattern — *tiny free float × lockup overhang × crowded short × options gamma × a legit-sounding narrative = a violent round-trip that is a re-rating, not a re-valuation* — rhymes exactly with the KB's other float anti-lessons: **BYND 2019** (post-IPO minuscule float + "future of protein" TAM), **GME 2021** (the squeeze taken to its extreme; TLRY was GME three years early, minus the message boards), and **de-SPAC squeezes 2021** (redemptions shrank floats to slivers). To tell a mania re-rate from an earned one: (1) **follow the float, not the market cap** — a big cap on a tiny tradeable float is a red flag, and you must ask the lockup calendar; (2) **look for a fundamental delta** roughly the size of the move (TLRY's business changed ~0% while the stock did 17x); (3) **read the tape's fingerprints** (volatility halts, ±40% days, intraday round-trips, vanished borrow, call volume dwarfing shares); (4) **distrust the catalyst-stack** (legit-but-immaterial headlines get retrofitted onto a move mechanics already caused). And the number that hides the wound: TLRY finished 2018 **~4x its IPO price** — a line that reads like triumph and buries a **-75%+ drawdown from the peak**. **Point-to-point returns launder path**; in mania names the peak-to-trough path *is* the story, and it is where real capital was destroyed. Tilray's later fate (a shadow of the peak, merged with Aphria 2021) confirmed the 2018 price was never value — it was mechanics wearing a legalization costume.

### AMRN — Amarin Corporation plc (NASDAQ: AMRN)

**Headline:** ~$2.99 (Sep 21, 2018 close) → ~$14–19 by late 2018; roughly a **4–5x intraday spike / +300–415% peak** and **+239% on the calendar year**; starting market cap ~**$1.0–1.3B**, exiting 2018 at ~**$5–6B**. The single cleanest *business-change* re-rate of 2018 — one drug, one trial, one date — and the deliberate opposite of TLRY.

**What the market misunderstood.** For most of its life Amarin was a punchline: its sole product **Vascepa (icosapent ethyl)** was a purified ethyl ester of EPA — "the good omega-3" — and to a generalist, *it's just fish oil.* The approved 2012 "MARINE" label covered only severe hypertriglyceridemia (≥500 mg/dL), a niche; the company burned cash, diluted, fought the FDA over promotion, and traded near **$3** (~$1B cap) into September 2018, priced as a commodity supplement. The skepticism was *evidence-based*, which is why the unwind was violent: in **2018 alone** two large omega-3 outcomes trials — **ASCEND** and **VITAL** — reported **no meaningful CV benefit** (both low-dose ~1g/day mixed EPA/DHA). Consensus priced Amarin's **REDUCE-IT** as a coin flip weighted toward failure. The crucial miss: REDUCE-IT was a **different drug at a different dose in a different population** — **4 g/day of pure EPA** in **statin-treated patients with elevated triglycerides (135–499) and established CVD/diabetes+risk** — exactly the high-risk on-statin residual-risk group where a real benefit would be enormous. The bearish read-through conflated "fish-oil supplements" with "high-dose prescription EPA," and that category-level prior swamping a product-level distinction *was* the mispricing.

**What actually changed — business vs re-rating.** *Business change (real, fundamental, exogenous):* on **September 24, 2018**, REDUCE-IT hit its primary endpoint — an **~25% relative risk reduction in MACE** (CV death, MI, stroke, revascularization, unstable angina), **p<0.001**, across **8,179 patients** over a median ~4.9 years. That is a hard clinical outcome — fewer heart attacks and strokes — that instantly and irreversibly expanded Vascepa's addressable population from a ≥500-mg/dL niche to **tens of millions** of statin-treated elevated-TG secondary-prevention patients; the 25% RRR blew past the ~15% whisper. *Re-rating change (mechanical, downstream):* short covering (a crowded short thesis invalidated overnight), a TAM re-rate (targets doubled $10→$20 the same day; multi-billion and even ~$20B bull cases), and a **new comp set** (from struggling specialty-pharma to owner of a branded CV blockbuster). The stock spiked ~415% in September and closed 2018 **+239%**. The distinction that matters: here the **fundamentals genuinely moved first**, and the short/TAM/multiple effects were *consequences* of a dated, statistically-significant de-risking event — not the cause. That is why it is the "clean" re-rate.

**Mechanism & timeline.** Pre-catalyst (through Sep 21): ~$3, ~$1B cap, heavy shorts, negative ASCEND/VITAL read-through → **Sep 24 topline** (25% MACE RRR, p<0.001) gaps the stock up violently; this is the re-rate date → **Nov 10 AHA + simultaneous NEJM** publication confirms/strengthens the data (a mineral-oil-placebo debate capped but didn't break the thesis) → year-end holds most of the gain (~$14–19, ~$5–6B cap, +239%). Epilogue (instructive, not the re-rate): **Dec 13 2019** FDA broad CV-risk-reduction label expansion (the commercial unlock the re-rate front-ran) → **Mar 30 2020** U.S. District Court (Nevada) invalidates the key Vascepa patents (obviousness) vs Hikma/Dr. Reddy's, **Federal Circuit affirms Sept 2020**, generics enter, and the stock is gutted.

**Transferable lesson.** The pattern — *priced-as-a-commodity, de-risked by a hard outcome* — has extreme asymmetry when the market has written a business off as structurally cheap ("it's just fish oil") and a **single, dated, binary event can produce an objective, hard-to-argue result** (a MACE outcomes trial is about as hard as clinical evidence gets): the downside was already in the ~$1B price, the upside was a genuine step-change in TAM, and the negative consensus rested on a **mis-applied analogy** (low-dose mixed omega-3 failures read across to high-dose pure EPA). Re-rates of this type live in the gap between "the category doesn't work" and "*this specific version* works." **The essential caveat the KB must keep: clinical success ≠ commercial moat.** Amarin is simultaneously the model of a clean, real, fundamental business-change re-rate *and* a warning that a fully validated business-change can be unwound by a **different risk axis entirely** — REDUCE-IT was never disputed as science, the label expanded, the drug worked; what killed the thesis was **patent/IP law**. When you underwrite a "de-risked by a hard outcome" re-rate you have retired the scientific/regulatory risk but said nothing about the **durability of the economics** — patent life, exclusivity and litigation are a separate leg of the stool, and a single-asset company with a contestable patent estate is one adverse docket entry from having its franchise value evaporate no matter how well the drug performs. A real business change *earns* the re-rate; only a defensible moat lets you *keep* it.

### IIPR — Innovative Industrial Properties

**Magnitude: ~2.3–2.7x confirmed in calendar 2018 (roughly $18-20 → $45-48), a clean ≥2x — the "largely flat" scout claim was wrong.** Exact daily close-to-close data was unreachable (price-history sites 403-blocked), so treat the precise multiple as approximate, but it is well-anchored by hard SEC-filed facts: IIPR priced a follow-on equity offering at **$26.00/share on January 18, 2018**, then another at **$40.00/share on October 3, 2018** (+54% in under nine months, on *primary-offering* prices — not even intraday highs), consistent with a further push into the mid-$40s by year-end. Separately, IIPR was named one of only ~13 cannabis-related stocks that *gained* in 2018 — a year when the broader cannabis-mania cohort (built on the Jan-1-2018 California rec-legalization rally) mostly gave back its gains. That is enough to confirm a genuine 2018 re-rating, not a 2019-2020-only story.

What the market missed early: a boring sale-leaseback landlord looked like the least exciting way to play cannabis next to the flashy Canadian LPs — but IIPR's structure (buy the cultivator's real estate, lease it back, collect contractual rent, never touch the plant) dodged federal Schedule-I banking/plant-touching risk entirely while capturing a capital-starved industry's desperation for financing. The business change was real and compounding: rental revenue +111% YoY and AFFO/share +344% YoY by Q4 2018, funded by the same equity offerings ($26→$40) that priced in a widening rent roll and dividend growth each quarter. On top came a re-rating premium — scarcity value as the only listed cannabis-dedicated REIT plus a "picks-and-shovels" narrative that let institutions get exposure without touching the plant. **Lesson: in a mania, the "arms dealer" with real, growing cash flows re-rates more durably than the story stocks — IIPR HELD and kept compounding through 2018-2019 while branded-cannabis names like TLRY round-tripped their entire 2018 spike.** (Re-tiered to 2-3x on the verified ~2.5x magnitude — kept as the durable counterpoint to the cannabis anti-lessons.)

### MDGL — Madrigal Pharmaceuticals (EXCLUDED — intra-year 3.3x peak round-tripped to ~+22% close)

**Not a clean calendar-2018 ≥2x — kept here as an anti-lesson.** MDGL produced 2018's most dramatic biotech chart and is the biotech twin of TLRY's "point-to-point launders path" lesson. The facts: NASH (now MASH) was the most-hyped, most-cursed indication in biotech — a silent epidemic, tens of millions of patients, **zero** approved drugs, TAMs of $20-35B+, and a graveyard of failed molecules (Intercept's OCA raised LDL and itched; GENFIT's elafibranor later failed Phase 3). Madrigal's **MGL-3196 (resmetirom)** — a liver-directed, **β-selective thyroid-hormone-receptor (THR-β) agonist** — was differentiated on exactly the axis that sinks NASH drugs: it *lowered* atherogenic lipids and was well tolerated. In 2018 it delivered three de-risking readouts (Feb 8 HeFH lipid Phase 2; **May 31 NASH Phase 2 MRI-PDFF −36.3%/−42.0% vs −9.6% placebo → +121% intraday**; Nov 12 AASLD Presidential Plenary + *Lancet* histology, 27% vs 6% NASH resolution) and the stock spiked ~3.3x into the low-$300s on a tiny ~12-15M-share float.

**Why it's excluded:** the stock **opened 2018 near ~$92** (it had *already* re-rated once off its ~$30 / ~$330M 2017 base on a Sept-2017 interim readout — that pre-2018 leg is where the "~8-10x" figure lives), and the widely-cited **+227% was H1-only, to the peak**. Once the micro-float's marginal forced-buyer was exhausted, it mean-reverted, **closing Dec 31 2018 at $112.72 — only ~+22% on the calendar year**, well short of the clean ≥2x bar. **The transferable lessons survive the exclusion:** (1) the biggest *intra-year* moves come from de-risking a "hopeless-indication" on a tiny float — hopeless-expectations × micro-float × one clean readout that flips the base rate; (2) the variant view was *mechanistic, not promotional* — the winner in a field of look-alike lottery tickets was identifiable ex-ante by which asset dodged the specific liability (lipids/tolerability) that killed the incumbents; (3) but a **micro-float re-rate overshoots and round-trips**, so the durable money was recognizing the de-risk and holding the long, volatile arc to approval — **Rezdiffra became the first FDA-approved NASH/MASH drug in March 2024** — not marking the intraday $300 as fair value. MDGL is the reminder that *being right about the business* and *clearing a clean calendar-year 2x at the close* are two different tests.
